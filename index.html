Research bulletin · mechanistic interpretability of medical language · gemma-2-2b

Patients don’t speak like doctors.
Medical AI must be ready to interpret them.

We trace how gemma-2-2b reads clinical terms versus the everyday words patients use. Wording can change what the model suggests in addition to its confidence: “my asthma flares, so at work I keep a spare” continues with inhaler at 69% probability; “my chest gets all tight” makes the top word shirt, and inhaler falls to 4% probability. Rewriting the sentence in clinical terms restores it. (Scenario 85 of the simulated series.)

Every figure is a live render: columns are the prompt’s words in order; height is depth in the model; the predicted next word sits at the top. Nodes (the dots) are features — size is contribution, color is category. Curves are paths of influence; the spread at the top shows the competing recommendations ranked by probability. Hover any node for its identity and mass.

Safety view — urgency of the predicted actiontiers reviewed v1

Not every flip is equal. Sometimes the model hedges (e.g. the clinical answer stays on top but loses probability). Sometimes it redirects: the top answer changes, and when it lands on a lower tier of care (specialist → generalist, medication → nothing) we call that a recommendation downgrade.
