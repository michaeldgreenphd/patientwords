Methods · how the pipeline works

From a sentence to a circuit, in four steps.

Every figure in the gallery starts as two (or four) plain sentences, one phrased the way a clinician would write it and one the way a patient would say it, and ends as an interactive map of the computation gemma-2-2b performs on each. Here is what happens in between, in plain language.

Step 1 · Model & features

gemma-2-2b, read through Gemma Scope transcoders

The subject of the study is gemma-2-2b, an open-weights language model. On its own, its internal activity is millions of undifferentiated numbers. Google DeepMind’s Gemma Scope transcoders re-express that activity as a sparse set of features: individually meaningful units that tend to fire for one recognizable thing, such as mentions of mental-health treatment, first-person symptom talk, or the syntax of a quoted sentence.

Step 2 · Attribution

Tracing the circuit with circuit-tracer, via Neuronpedia

For each prompt we run circuit-tracer through Neuronpedia to build an attribution graph: a causal map of which features, at which layers and token positions, actually pushed the model toward its next-token prediction. Edges carry attribution mass, the amount one feature’s firing contributed to another’s and ultimately to the final logits. The graph records what the model actually did on this exact sentence, at every layer and every token position of the run.

Step 3 · Tagging

Sorting features into clinical, off-target, and structural

Each feature in a graph is tagged by what it responds to, using its autointerp description and activation profile:

  • Clinical: features that belong to the medical computation we want: the concept of depression, therapy, diagnosis, treatment.
  • Off-target: features dragged in by the colloquial wording: idiom, mood-as-weather metaphors, music senses of “the blues.” These are the mechanism of the language penalty.
  • Structural: scaffolding features for syntax, position, and punctuation that appear in every trace and carry no medical content.

This three-way split is what makes two graphs comparable: when the wording changes, you can see clinical mass drain away and off-target mass take its place.

Step 4 · Comparison

Three engines for putting graphs side by side

The tagged graphs feed three rendering engines, each answering a different question:

  • Word Differences 2panel · Fig. 1

    What does one word cost? Two stacked traces of the same sentence, differing only in “depression” vs. “the blues.”

  • Syntax Differences 4quadrant · Fig. 2

    Which part of patient language does the damage? A 2×2 grid crossing the medical keyword with the surrounding frame, separating the vocabulary effect from the syntax effect.

  • Dialect Differences dialect · Fig. 3

    Which communities pay the framing tax? The clinical term is held fixed while the surrounding syntax is re-traced across dialect and register variants, extending the single syntax delta into a distribution across dialects.

  • Translation translation · Fig. 4

    Can the penalty be undone? An LLM translates the patient sentence into clinical terms, and the translated output is traced natively to show the circuit and the prediction recovering together.

Every render is a single self-contained HTML file: hover any node for its feature identity, category, attribution mass, and autointerp description.
