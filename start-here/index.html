Start here · the whole idea in five minutes

People say the same thing many ways. What does a machine "hear"?

1 · A doctor converges

Three phrasings, one interpretation

Patients rarely use words that appear in medical textbooks. A clinician understands multiple different framings, recognizes the same stomach problem, and reaches the same recommendation.

interpretation absorbs the wording

2 · The model diverges

Same three phrasings, three different answers

We gave a language model the same sentences and read off its most likely next word. The clinical phrasing makes a medical recommendation. The casual phrasings make a food recommendation.

the wording chooses the answer — measured on gemma-2-2b · see the traces: stomach’s on fire · dyspepsia ladder

4 · Look inside

Circuit tracing photographs the reasoning

Inside the model, small units called "features" switch on as it reads — some respond to medical ideas, others to everyday ones. A circuit trace records which features fired and how strongly each fed the final recommendation. Clinical wording lights the medical features; patient wording lights the everyday ones.

node size = how strongly the feature fed the guess · faint dots = features that barely fired

Most figures on this site are circuit tracings like this one.

5 · What we measure

Two numbers per sentence pair

How much confidence the right answer lost when the wording was more similar to patient language, and whether the top answer changed. A changed answer that points to less help — a recommendation for an "inhaler" becoming a recommendation for a "shirt" shows how some of these differences present themselves.

6 · The patch

A translation layer, for now

Put a translator between the patient and the model: it rewrites the sentence into clinical wording before the model answers.

ice cream (29%) becomes antacid (31%) after translation. Importantly for now this is a patch, not a permanent solution: it fixed 8 of the 20 hardest cases, left 7 unchanged, and once made things worse · see the live translation traces

7 · The real fix

Teach the listener, not just the speaker

Two paths lead past the patch.

Context up front

One plain sentence placing the conversation in a medical setting roughly halved the damage from casual wording.

Train on patient language

Models learn idioms, misspellings, and dialects the way clinicians do.
